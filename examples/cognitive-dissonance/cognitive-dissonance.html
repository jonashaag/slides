Cognitive Dissonance

One of the most extensively studied theories in social psychology, confirmed in hundreds of experiments

History

  • Leon Festinger (social psychologist in NY) infiltrated (Observation by Participation) an UFO sect in the 1950s
  • the sect expected the end of the world by a certain date
  • the prediction failed, but the members started to evangelize other people instead of breaking up

Cognitive Dissonance

Sect members had two contradicting cognitions:

  • “According to our believes, the world should have ended yesterday”
  • “The world still exists”

These people had cognitive dissonance.

Cognitive Dissonance in General

An uncomfortable feeling caused by multiple dissonant cognitions, e.g. if

  • your behaviour does not match your moral concepts
  • you made a decision and there were other comparable alternatives
  • you made a wrong decision

Resolving Cognitive Dissonance

Strategy 1: Change one cognition to match the other

Change behaviour to match values (unlikely) or change values to match behaviour (more likely)

Strategy 2: Downplay the dissonance

Disappointed after note issue: “10/15 pts suffice”

Strategy 3: Involve external excuses

“I was forced to act/everybody acts this way”

The Fox and the Grapes

The Fox and the Grapes

  • the Fox has two clashing cognitions:
    I want to eat those grapes
    and
    I can not reach them
  • to resolve the dissonance, the Fox choses strategy 1 by changing the first cognition: “The grapes are not ripe yet anyway”
  • The Fox and the Grapes is a fable, so this has to be considered metaphorical

Dissonance After Placing a Bet

  • Bettors (horse race) were asked to rate the horse the selected immediately before and after placing the bet
  • they rated their horse 50% better than before the bet (on a scale from 1 to 7)
  • strategy 1

Benjamin Franklin Effect

  • B.J. asked a political opponent the favor of borrowing him a book (by post)
  • opponent sent him the book
  • next time they met he talked gently to B.J. for the first time and they became friends
  • opponent had cognitive dissonance (“I don't like B.J.” vs. “I did him a favor”)
  • strategy 1

Deficient Reward Paradigm

  • probands spent an hour on very boring activities
  • then they were asked to suggest other people that the task was very interesting
  • some of them were paid $7, others $150
  • the $7 group rated the task as much more interesting than the $150 group
  • strategy 1 ↔ strategy 3

Deficient Punishment Paradigm

  • children (kindergarden) were forbidden to play with their favorite toy
  • half the children were threatened with severe, the other half with mild punishment
  • the severe punishment group continued to use their favorite toys after the experiment
  • the other half did not
  • strategy 1 ↔ strategy 3

Hand-Washing Reduces Post-Decisional Cognitive Dissonance

  • probands chose between one of two CDs (they like both)
  • half the probands washed their hands after the choice
  • the other half rated the selected CD much higher(strategy 1) than the hand-washing group
  • conclusion: hand-washing reduces post-decisional cognitive dissonance
